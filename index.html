Writing Against
AI Slop

Practice exercises for Technical and Professional Communication

I'm Calvin Pollak, an Assistant Teaching Professor of Technical and Professional Communication at the University of Washington. In this project, I'm drawing from recent research on the peculiar language style of AI-generated text to design practice exercises for my Technical and Professional Communication students.

The exercises are designed to give students practice recognizing "AI slop" language features and writing and editing to make their language sound more human, particularly in cover letters, instruction manuals, and proposals.

Research Background

The style contrast underlying these exercises is drawn from two corpus linguistics studies. Markey et al. (2024) found that ChatGPT-generated text is significantly more informationally dense than even professional academic writing; it relies heavily on nominalizations and noun phrases, and is low on the hedges, modals of possibility, and co-ordinating conjunctions that keep human prose open and dialogic. Reinhart et al. (2025) extended this analysis to multiple LLMs and found that instruction-tuned models use present participial clauses at 2–5× the human rate, nominalizations at 1.5–2× the human rate, and systematically avoid clausal co-ordination relative to human writers, and these findings hold across genres and model sizes.

Markey, B., Brown, D. W., Laudenbach, M., & Kohler, A. (2024). Dense and disconnected: Analyzing the sedimented style of ChatGPT-generated text at scale. Written Communication, 41(4), 571–600.

Reinhart, A., Markey, B., Laudenbach, M., Pantusen, K., Yurko, R., Weinberg, G., & Brown, D. W. (2025). Do LLMs write like humans? Variation in grammatical and rhetorical styles. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 122(8), e2422455122.
