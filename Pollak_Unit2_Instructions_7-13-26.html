Course Unit 2 · Instructional Design
More Human Instructions
A small-group activity: have an LLM write instructions for a tool your group knows well, take the instructions apart by asking three kinds of questions, and then rewrite them the way you'd explain the tool to a friend or family member.

What you'll do and why. Instructional writing is where AI-inflected style does the most practical damage, because a confused reader can't complete the task. The best test of instructions is a knowledgeable insider: someone who knows the tool well enough to notice where the document skips things, gets the order wrong, or dresses simple actions up in formal language. That's your group. You'll pick a tool you all know, get an LLM to document it, and evaluate the result the way real users would.

By the end your group should be able to:

  • analyze instructions for their language style, supplementary discussion, and formatting;
  • explain what good instructional writers add beyond a bare list of steps;
  • rewrite a flawed section so a genuine novice could follow it.
Part 1Generate the instructions
Groups of 3–4. Budget about 10 minutes for this part.
Agree on a piece of software, an app, or a tool that everyone in your group knows well: a game you all play, an app you all use daily, a lab tool, a campus system. Insider knowledge is the point.
In the university-licensed LLM chatbot, prompt: "Write instructions for installing and getting started with [your tool] for someone who has never used it." Don't add style guidance.
Read the output together once, silently, before discussing. Note your first reactions individually.
Prefer not to use AI? That's always an option in this course. If your group opts out (or can't agree on a shared tool), you can analyze the previously-generated example below instead. It documents an app that most students know, so your insider knowledge still applies.

Getting Started with Zoom: Installation and Initial Configuration

Part 2Analyze by asking three kinds of questions
Assign one question set per group member (double up in groups of four), discuss together, and record your findings. Notes stay on your screen only; copy them out before closing the page.
Question set 1
Language style: is it dense and disconnected to the point of confusing the user?
  • Hunt for nominalizations that bury the action the user must take ("the execution of the installer file" vs. "double-click the installer"). Who is the subject of each step, and is it the reader?
  • Find the densest noun phrase. Would a first-time user know what to physically do after reading it?
  • Where would a plain imperative verb ("click," "type," "wait") have been clearer?
Question set 2
Supplementary discussion: does it explain things the way you would for a friend or family member?
  • When you help someone install this tool in person, what do you warn them about? Is any of that here?
  • Good instructions explain why a step matters, flag what can go wrong, and say what success looks like ("you'll see a green checkmark"). Where is this missing?
  • Your insider knowledge: what did the document get wrong, skip, or describe in a way no actual user would recognize?
Question set 3
Formatting: does the layout match the logic of the task?
  • Sequential steps should be numbered, because order matters and users need to keep their place. Did the document use bullets where it should have used numbers?
  • Is each step one action, or are several actions packed into one item?
  • Where would a screenshot, a caution box, or a "before you begin" list have helped?
Discussion checkpoint: as a group, decide which set of questions revealed the most serious problem. Style issues make instructions unpleasant to read, but which failures make them actually unusable?
Part 3Rewrite it
Together, rewrite one full section (installation is usually the best candidate) so a genuine novice could follow it. Requirements: numbered steps, one action per step, the reader as the subject of every instruction, at least one tip or warning drawn from your group's real experience with the tool, and a "what you'll see" detail confirming success.
Tip: this page doesn't save your work. Copy your rewrite into a shared document before closing the tab.
Part 4Compare and reflect
Put the original and the rewrite side by side and answer together:
  • What knowledge did your rewrite depend on that the LLM couldn't have had?
  • Which differences are style fixes, and which are content the model simply didn't know to include?
  • The LLM produced its version in seconds and yours took twenty minutes. For which audiences and situations is the twenty-minute version worth it, and are there any where it isn't?
Bring to class / submit (one per group)
  • The generated instructions (or the previously-generated example) with your notes on all three question sets.
  • Your rewritten section.
  • Your Part 4 reflection answers.
